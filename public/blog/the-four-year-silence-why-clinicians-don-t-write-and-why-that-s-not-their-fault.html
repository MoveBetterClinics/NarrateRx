The Four-Year Silence: Why Clinicians Don't Write (And Why That's Not Their Fault)

We had an employee for over four years who never published a single piece of content. Not one. Not for lack of asking — we asked repeatedly, over and over. We scheduled time for it. We carved out blocks in the calendar. We did the thing every clinic owner is told to do: make space, give people permission, hold them accountable.

Nothing happened. For four years.

And when I finally sat down with this person and really asked — what is going on here — the answer was simple. Writing just isn't their strong suit. That was it. Not a motivation problem. Not a priorities problem. Not a "let me try harder next quarter" problem. They just weren't a writer. And the more I thought about it, the more I realized — why would they be?

The patient room versus the blank page

Here's what made the whole thing absurd to me. I would listen to these same clinicians talk to their patients about their ideas — what we were trying to do medically, how movement worked, why we framed care the way we did — and the content was right there. Fully formed. Articulate. Specific. The kind of stuff that, if you transcribed it, you'd publish.

But ask them to sit down at a laptop and write 600 words about low back pain, and they freeze. And honestly, fair. Low back pain can mean a hundred things. Where do you even start? The patient in front of them was always the springboard — a real person with a real problem gave them a frame to talk inside of. Take the patient away, hand them a blinking cursor, and the whole thing collapses.

That's not a character flaw. That's a medium problem. Writing is its own skill set — organizing your thoughts into written words, structuring a piece, making it land elegantly. That is a thing people train for. And clinicians didn't train for it.

Clinicians are trained to be clinicians

This is the part I want every clinic owner to sit with for a second, because I think we've collectively gaslit ourselves into thinking otherwise.

Clinicians are trained to be clinicians. You're not trained to be writers. You're not trained to run social media. You're not trained to run a business. Your job — the most important job — is to help people. That's what you went to school for. That's the road you went down.

They didn't go down the road of "I wanna write books" — that's what they would be doing if that was the issue. They went down the road of becoming a doctor because they wanted to be a doctor. And somewhere along the way, the climate shifted and being a great doctor stopped being enough. Now you also need to be a great business owner, a great marketer, a great social media creator, a great brand voice. All the addendums got piled on top of the actual job.

It's suffocating. And it's especially suffocating when you're small. A large clinic can defer some of this cost — hire an internal marketing person, build a content team, run the whole operation in-house. Great, fine, that works. But that's not the reality for most clinics. The margins are too thin. There's no budget line for "internal content department." So the work falls back on the clinician, who didn't sign up for it, isn't trained in it, and is already booked solid seeing patients.

And then we're surprised when four years go by and nothing gets posted.

The medium is the problem, not the person

Here's the reframe I want to leave you with, because I think this is where most clinic owners get it wrong — myself included for a long time.

When your clinicians aren't producing content, the instinct is to blame the clinician. They're not motivated. They don't see the value. They need more accountability, a better calendar block, a tighter deadline. We tried all of that. None of it worked. And the reason none of it worked is that we were solving the wrong problem.

The problem isn't the clinician. The problem is that we were asking them to do their work in the wrong medium.

Think about what we actually do all day. We talk. The whole business — chiropractic, PT, naturopathic, acupuncture, massage, all of it — is a verbal business. From the first second you meet a patient, it's a conversation. You explain things with words, out loud, in real time. You never sit a patient down and hand them a written essay about their care plan. That's not how this works. Your chart notes aren't how you communicate with patients.

So when we tell a clinician "go write a blog post," we're asking them to take everything they normally do in one medium — speech, in conversation, in response to a real person — and transfer it into a completely different medium they were never trained in and never signed up for. Of course it doesn't happen. Of course it sits on the to-do list for four years.

The talking was always there. The ideas were always there. The voice was always there. What was missing was a way to capture what already comes out naturally, without forcing the clinician to become a writer first.

What this means if you run a clinic

If you're a clinic owner reading this and you've been frustrated that your team isn't producing content — stop blaming your team. They're not broken. They're not lazy. They're not unmotivated. They are doing the job you hired them to do, which is seeing patients and helping people.

If you've tried scheduling time for them to write and nothing came of it, the answer isn't more scheduled time. If you've tried buying them a course on writing, that's not it either. And — I'll get into this more in Part 2 of this series — if you've tried outsourcing to a marketing agency, you probably already know that didn't fix it either, just in a different way.

The real answer is to stop asking clinicians to be writers. Meet them where they already are, which is in conversation. The content you need already exists — it lives in the way your clinicians talk to patients every single day. I'll get into the mechanics of how you actually pull it out of them in Part 3.

For now, the takeaway is just this: the four-year silence in your clinic isn't a people problem. It's a medium problem. And once you see it that way, the whole thing stops being frustrating and starts being solvable.
